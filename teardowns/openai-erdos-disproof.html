OpenAI: Erdős Unit Distance Disproof

OpenAI — May 20, 2026 — Internal reasoning model (speculated GPT-5.6)

🔗 Official announcement

Research + Code 🟡 Track 18/20
TL;DR: A general-purpose unreleased OpenAI reasoning model autonomously disproved the 80-year-old Erdős unit distance conjecture — the first time AI has solved a prominent open research problem. The proof used unexpected tools from algebraic number theory (Golod–Shafarevich theory, infinite class field towers), was verified by Fields medalist Tim Gowers and other leading mathematicians, and cost <$1,000 in compute. This marks a qualitative jump from competition math (IMO Gold) to open research.

The problem

The planar unit distance problem, posed by Paul Erdős in 1946: if you place n points in the plane, how many pairs can be exactly distance 1 apart? For 80 years, mathematicians believed the “square grid” construction was essentially optimal, yielding growth of n1+C/log log(n).

What the model did

The model constructed an infinite family of configurations achieving n1+δ unit-distance pairs for a fixed δ > 0, disproving the conjecture. Princeton’s Will Sawin later refined δ = 0.014.

Key takeaway

This is not a competition problem with a known answer. It’s an open research problem where no solution previously existed. The model found one autonomously.

What's Actually New

▼

Evidence quality: STRONG

Verified by Fields medalist Tim Gowers, Thomas Bloom, Noga Alon (Princeton), Arul Shankar. Will Sawin (Princeton) independently refined the result to δ = 0.014. Companion paper published.

Genuine novelty

  • Open problem, not competition problem — qualitative jump from IMO-level math
  • Cross-domain insight: algebraic number theory (Golod–Shafarevich theory, infinite class field towers) applied to a geometry question nobody expected these tools to reach
  • General-purpose model — not a math-specific system, not scaffolded, not AlphaProof-style
  • 125-page reasoning chain = sustained long-horizon coherence
  • <$1,000 compute for a research-grade result

Recycled packaging

  • “AI does math” narrative is familiar (AlphaProof, IMO Gold)
  • The model itself isn’t new — it’s an unreleased internal model
  • Disproof via counterexample construction is less impressive than a constructive proof or new theory

The “So What”

▼
IF THIS IS REAL, WHAT CHANGES?

Test-time compute scaling just got its killer demo. The “inference is the new training” thesis has a concrete proof point. Models that can think for 32 hours and produce verified novel results are a new tool category — not chatbots, not agents, but research collaborators.

IF WE IGNORE THIS, WHAT'S THE RISK?

Low near-term product risk (math proofs ≠ consumer features). Medium strategic risk: if OpenAI’s reasoning frontier is producing research-grade results, their foundation models may be pulling ahead in ways that compound across domains.

Action item

Track. No immediate action required. But the centaur pattern holds: the model did the search, the humans (Gowers, Sawin) did the verification and refinement. Operator skill still matters. Research labs should assess their roadmap for extended test-time compute in scientific reasoning.

Teardown Quiz — 10 Questions
1. [Section 1] What type of system produced the proof?
OpenAI emphasized this was a general-purpose reasoning model, not a domain-specific math system like AlphaProof. It was not scaffolded to search through proof strategies or targeted at the unit distance problem in particular.
2. [Section 2] What is the key qualitative jump from the IMO Gold result to this achievement?
IMO Gold solved competition problems that humans had already solved. This result solved an open research problem where no prior solution existed — a qualitatively different achievement, not just more of the same.
3. [Section 3] What makes the proof’s use of algebraic number theory significant?
The tools (Golod–Shafarevich theory, infinite class field towers) were well-known to algebraic number theorists, but nobody expected them to have implications for geometric questions in the Euclidean plane. The novelty is in the cross-domain connection, not the tools themselves.
4. [Section 4] What is the most accurate characterization of the model’s technical approach?
OpenAI disclosed almost nothing about the model’s technical details. The speculated “GPT-5.6” name comes from external observers. The companion paper was written by mathematicians verifying the result, not describing the model.
5. [Section 5] Why is the “disproof” classification an important limitation flag?
While disproofs are absolutely valid mathematical contributions, constructing a counterexample is generally considered less demanding than building new theory or proving positive results. This nuance matters when assessing the significance of the achievement.
6. [Section 6] How does this launch connect to AI-powered creative generation?
The connection is indirect but real: the ability to connect distant knowledge areas (algebraic number theory → geometry) mirrors the creative generation challenge of connecting user intent, aesthetic principles, and constraints in novel combinations. But it’s a weak connection, not a direct one.
7. [Section 6] Which research area receives the strongest competitive signal from this launch?
The result directly signals that OpenAI’s reasoning capabilities have reached a qualitatively new level. The strongest competitive signal goes to reasoning/RL teams at other labs, because this demonstrates capability that no competitor has publicly matched.
8. [Section 7] What is the assessed competitive moat for this result?
The moat is talent combined with compute. Distribution doesn’t matter for a research result — there are no “users” to distribute to. What matters is having a model capable of sustained reasoning AND the willingness to spend 32 hours of compute on a single problem.
9. [Section 8] What is the recommended action item?
The recommended action is “Track.” No immediate action is required because the near-term product risk is low. But it’s a strategic signal that warrants assessing whether labs are investing in extended test-time compute for scientific reasoning, where OpenAI appears to be pulling ahead.
10. [Sections 9+10] Why does the scorecard assign 5/5 for both Novelty and Signal Value?
Novelty 5/5 because this is literally the first time AI has solved a prominent open research problem — that’s maximally novel by definition. Signal Value 5/5 because the result validates two major field-wide trends: test-time compute scaling produces qualitatively new capabilities, and general-purpose models outperform specialized ones for surprising results.
